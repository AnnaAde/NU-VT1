Introduction

From 2015 to 2021, the amount of music streaming subscribers has increased by 540% worldwide (Statista). The United States (U.S.) and the United Kingdom (U.K.) are expected to be the most prominent streaming markets in the next years, however Brazil, India, China, and the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) have potential to catch up to be among the top markets as early as 2026. Spotify is the leading music streaming service, taking up over 30 percent of market share globally.

In 2020, music consumption decreased on average 12.5 percent after the World Health Organization (WHO) declared COVID-19 outbreak a pandemic. Specifically, Spotify lost $838 million of revenue in the first three quarters of 2020 (Carnegie Mellon University). Despite an overall drop in music streaming in 2020, there was an increase of 87 million subscribers in the past year (Statista). I will explore the top 50 tracks of 2020 and compare any commonalities between artists and genres.

The dataset used includes 16 variables on the top 50 songs on Spotify in 2020. The data was derived from Spotify’s ranking for the most played songs from 1.1.2020-31.12.2021. Information about each song's artist, the album it's on, and genre is included. In addition, the following variables are included (hover for definitions):

Energy

Higher value means that the songs are more energetic

Danceability

Higher value means that it is easier to dance to the song

Key

The primary identifier of songs. Main group of pitches or notes

Loudness

Higher value means that the song is louder

Acousticness

This value describes how acoustic a song is

Speechiness

Speechiness detects the presence of spoken words in a track

Instrumentalness

Describes whether a track contains no vocals. If the instrumentalness is closer to '1', the song is more likely to have no vocals

Liveness

Describes the likelihood that the song was recorded with a live audience

Valence

Describes the positiveness of a song. Higher valence means that the song sounds more positive

Tempo

Beats per minute

Duration

The length of the song (milliseconds)

Exploratory Data Analysis

For interpreting this dataset, I was interested in learning about which artists and genres had many popular songs. I wanted to see if the artists with the most songs on the top 50 would also be artists in the genres that were present the most. Additionally, I compare the songs from the artists who have multiple tracks in this set to the top ten tracks overall. The genres are compared and analyzed to see if there are subgenres that link to a genre (16 total genres are listed in the dataset).

Findings from Visualizations

Top 50 Songs by Genre

Figure 1: This pie chart (right) shows the breakdown of the top songs by genre. The exact counts are on the labels. For percentage, double the counts. Example: Pop is 14/50, therefore (28/100) 28%.

  • 14 songs, or 28%, of songs were Pop songs, however 19 songs, or 38%, had pop subgenres (Example: Electro-Pop)
  • 13 songs, or 26%, of songs were Hip-Hop/Rap songs, however 16 songs, or 32%, includes subgenres (Example: Hip-Hop/Trap)
  • 5 songs, or 10%, of songs did not have specific Pop or Hip-Hop subgenres. Some may argue genres such as Dance/Electronic and Nu-Disco are close to Pop depending on the specific song

According to Statista, the top genres for music overall were R&B/Hip-Hop (31.1%), Rock (15.6%), and Pop (13.1%). The top 50 does not reflect close to these values especially with Rock being absent as a genre or subgenre. If the top 50 reflected the overall genre consumption, R&B/Hip-Hop would have 15 songs, Rock would have eight, and Pop would have six.

The pie chart format was used to show the proportions of the songs by percentage of a whole.

Comparing the Top Three Artists' Songs to the Top Ten

As seen with Statista’s numbers, the top ten songs appear to somewhat represent the overall genre consumption with R&B and Hip-Hop making up the majority of tracks (50%). Rock is still not present. Pop maintains a solid presence with 20%. Keeping in mind the top ten is compared to nine tracks for the top three artists, the Hip-Hop/Rap music appears the most with three tracks in both.

Turning to Billie Eilish, Dua Lipa, and Travis Scott, their music tends to stay within a genre. For Travis Scott, this is Hip-Hop/Rap. Dua Lipa’s music is mainly Dance and Disco and Billie Eilish’s tracks are Pop. Dua Lipa is the only artist to have a song in the top ten: “Don’t Start Now.” Its position is five with the Nu-Disco genre. There appears to be no patterns between the amount of times an artist shows up in the top charts with the genre their music is.
